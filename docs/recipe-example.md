# Tacos de Carnitas

> **Spanish:** Tacos de Carnitas Estilo Michoacán
> **English:** Michoacán-Style Carnitas Tacos
> **French:** Tacos de Carnitas à la Michoacán

## Overview / Resumen

Los tacos de carnitas son el orgullo de Michoacán. El cerdo se cocina lentamente en su propia grasa hasta quedar increíblemente tierno por dentro y crujiente por fuera. Servidos en tortillas de maíz calientes con cebolla, cilantro y una buena salsa verde, representan la esencia de la comida callejera mexicana.

**English:** Carnitas tacos are the pride of Michoacán. The pork is slowly cooked in its own fat until incredibly tender inside and crispy outside. Served on warm corn tortillas with onion, cilantro, and a good salsa verde, they represent the essence of Mexican street food.

### Ingredient Notes:

**Key Ingredient Relationships:**
> The combination of pork shoulder (lean) and pork belly (fatty) creates the perfect balance of tender meat and crispy bits. The milk helps tenderize the meat and adds richness.

**Substitutions:**
> - No pork belly? Use all pork shoulder but add 1/4 cup extra lard
> - No Mexican oregano? Mediterranean oregano works, use slightly less
> - No lard? Vegetable oil works but traditional flavor comes from lard

## Instructions / Instrucciones

### Step 1: Season the Pork / Sazonar el Cerdo
**Combine** the pork shoulder and pork belly in a large bowl. Season generously with salt, pepper, cumin, and oregano. Toss to coat evenly.

Let the meat sit at room temperature for 30 minutes while you prepare the other ingredients. This helps it cook more evenly.

> **Chef's Tip:** Don't skip the resting time. Cold meat straight from the fridge will cook unevenly and won't brown as well.

**Photo:** `carnitas-step-1.jpg` - Seasoned raw pork chunks in a bowl

---

### Step 2: Build the Braising Liquid / Preparar el Líquido
**Add** the lard to a large heavy-bottomed pot over medium heat. Once melted, add the seasoned pork, quartered onion, and halved garlic head.

**Pour** the orange juice and milk over the meat. Add the bay leaves. The liquid should come about halfway up the meat - don't submerge it completely.

> **Chef's Tip:** The orange juice adds brightness and helps tenderize. The milk proteins create amazing crispy bits at the end.

**Photo:** `carnitas-step-2.jpg` - Pork in pot with liquid and aromatics

---

### Step 3: Braise Low and Slow / Cocinar a Fuego Lento
**Bring** the liquid to a gentle simmer over medium heat. Then reduce heat to low, partially cover with a lid (leave a small gap), and cook for 2 to 2.5 hours.

The meat is ready when it's completely tender and falls apart easily when pressed with a fork. Internal temperature should be around 195-200°F (90-93°C).

> **Chef's Tip:** Resist the urge to stir frequently. Let the bottom pieces develop color. Stir gently only every 30-40 minutes.

**Photo:** `carnitas-step-3.jpg` - Meat simmering in pot, partially covered

---

### Step 4: Crisp the Carnitas / Dorar las Carnitas
**Remove** the lid completely. Increase heat to medium-high and continue cooking, stirring occasionally, until most of the liquid has evaporated (about 20-30 minutes).

Once the liquid is gone, the meat will begin to fry in the rendered fat. Continue cooking, turning the pieces occasionally, until the carnitas have crispy, caramelized edges all over, about 10-15 more minutes.

> **Chef's Tip:** This is the magic step! Don't rush it. Those crispy bits ("cuero") are the best part. Taste and adjust seasoning.

**Photo:** `carnitas-step-4.jpg` - Crispy golden carnitas in the pot

---

### Step 5: Rest and Shred / Reposar y Desmenuzar
**Transfer** the carnitas to a cutting board. Remove and discard the onion, garlic, and bay leaves. Let the meat rest for 5 minutes.

**Roughly chop** or shred the meat with two forks, keeping a mix of tender interior meat and crispy exterior pieces. Taste and adjust salt if needed.

> **Chef's Tip:** Keep some larger chunks for texture variety. The mix of shredded and chopped is traditional.

**Photo:** `carnitas-step-5.jpg` - Shredded carnitas on cutting board

---

### Step 6: Serve / Servir
**Warm** your corn tortillas on a dry comal or griddle until pliable and lightly charred in spots.

**Assemble** tacos by placing carnitas on doubled-up tortillas. Top with diced onion and cilantro. Serve immediately with lime wedges and salsa verde on the side.

> **Chef's Tip:** Double up the tortillas for street-style tacos - they're juicy! Squeeze lime over everything just before eating.

## Chef's Tips & Variations / Tips y Variaciones

### Tips:
1. **Use a mix of cuts:** The shoulder provides lean meat while the belly adds richness and helps create those crispy bits.
2. **Don't rush the crisping:** The final frying stage is what makes carnitas special. Be patient.
3. **Taste as you go:** Adjust seasoning after shredding when flavors are most concentrated.

### Variations:
- **Carnitas al Pastor:** Add 2 dried guajillo chiles and 1 canned chipotle to the braising liquid
- **Carnitas con Coca-Cola:** Replace half the orange juice with Mexican Coca-Cola for deeper color and subtle sweetness
- **Oven method:** Braise covered at 300°F (150°C) for 3 hours, then broil uncovered to crisp

### Make Ahead:
> Carnitas can be braised up to the crisping stage, cooled, and refrigerated for up to 3 days. The fat will solidify on top - this is perfect. Reheat in a skillet with some of that fat to re-crisp before serving.

### Leftovers:
> Store in an airtight container for up to 5 days. Reheat in a skillet with a splash of water and a bit of lard until crispy again. Also excellent in tortas, quesadillas, or huevos con carnitas.

## History & Background / Historia y Contexto

Las carnitas son originarias del estado de Michoacán, en el centro-oeste de México, donde se han preparado desde al menos el siglo XIX. Tradicionalmente, se cocinan en grandes cazos de cobre sobre fuego de leña, lo que les da un sabor ahumado único.

El nombre "carnitas" significa literalmente "little meats" en español, refiriéndose a los pequeños trozos de cerdo que resultan del proceso de cocción y desmenuzado. En Michoacán, las carnitas son un asunto serio - cada pueblo tiene su carnicero favorito, y las recetas familiares se guardan celosamente.

La técnica de confitar el cerdo en su propia grasa y luego freírlo hasta quedar crujiente es lo que distingue a las verdaderas carnitas michoacanas de simples preparaciones de cerdo desmenuzado. El resultado es una carne que es simultáneamente tierna, jugosa y crujiente - una contradicción deliciosa que solo se logra con paciencia y técnica.

**English:** Carnitas originate from the state of Michoacán in west-central Mexico, where they've been prepared since at least the 19th century. Traditionally, they're cooked in large copper pots over wood fire, giving them a unique smoky flavor. The technique of confiting the pork in its own fat and then frying it until crispy is what distinguishes true Michoacán carnitas from simple pulled pork preparations.

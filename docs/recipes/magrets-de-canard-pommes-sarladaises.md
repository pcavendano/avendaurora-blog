# Magrets de Canard Poêlés et Pommes de Terre Sarladaises

> **Spanish:** Magret de Pato Sellado con Papas Sarladaises
> **English:** Seared Duck Breast with Sarladaise Potatoes
> **French:** Magrets de Canard Poêlés et Pommes de Terre Sarladaises

## Overview / Resumen

Les magrets de canard poêlés sont un classique de la cuisine du Périgord, parfaits pour Noël ou les grandes occasions. Le canard est saisi pour obtenir une peau croustillante, accompagné de pommes de terre cuites dans la graisse de canard à la manière de Sarlat, le tout nappé d'une sauce au vin rouge et échalotes.

> Seared duck breast is a classic of Périgord cuisine, perfect for Christmas or special occasions. The duck is pan-seared for a crispy skin, accompanied by potatoes cooked in duck fat Sarlat-style, and finished with a red wine and shallot sauce.

**Key Ingredient Relationships:**

## Instructions / Instrucciones

### Step 1: Prepare the Sarladaise Potatoes / Préparer les Pommes Sarladaises
**Peel, slice, and cook:** Peel the Yukon Gold potatoes and slice them 1 cm thick. Rinse well and pat thoroughly dry.

Melt the duck fat in a large non-stick skillet over medium-low heat. Add the potatoes, cover, and cook for 15 minutes, turning them regularly to cook evenly.

Remove the lid, increase heat to medium-high, and continue cooking for about 15 minutes until golden and tender. Add the garlic and parsley, season with salt and pepper, and keep warm.

> **Chef's Tip:** Drying the potatoes thoroughly after rinsing prevents spattering and ensures a golden, crispy exterior in the duck fat.

**Photo:** `magrets-de-canard-pommes-sarladaises-step-1.jpg` - Golden potatoes cooking in duck fat in skillet

---

### Step 2: Make the Red Wine & Shallot Sauce / Préparer la Sauce
**Sauté and deglaze:** In a separate saucepan, brown the shallots in 30 ml of butter. Sprinkle with flour and cook for 1 minute, stirring constantly.

Deglaze with the red wine and reduce by half, about 4 minutes. Add the beef broth, tomato paste, molasses, and crushed peppercorns. Simmer until the sauce reduces to about 250 ml (1 cup). Adjust seasoning.

> **Chef's Tip:** The molasses adds a subtle depth and richness that complements the wine reduction beautifully. Don't skip it.

**Photo:** `magrets-de-canard-pommes-sarladaises-step-2.jpg` - Dark, glossy sauce reducing in pan

---

### Step 3: Sear the Duck Breasts / Saisir les Magrets
**Score and sear:** Using a sharp knife, score the duck skin in a crosshatch pattern, cutting through the fat but not into the meat. Season both sides with salt.

Place the duck breasts fat-side down in a cold skillet. Turn heat to medium and cook for about 10 minutes until the skin is deep golden and crispy, and most of the fat has rendered.

Flip and cook for 2 more minutes for rare (adjust to preference). Remove from skillet and rest for 10 minutes on a cutting board. Discard the rendered cooking fat (or save for another use).

> **Chef's Tip:** Starting the duck in a cold pan allows the fat to render slowly and evenly, resulting in the crispiest possible skin. Do not skip the resting time — it allows the juices to redistribute.

**Photo:** `magrets-de-canard-pommes-sarladaises-step-3.jpg` - Duck breast searing fat-side down with golden crispy skin

---

### Step 4: Cook the Mushrooms / Cuire les Champignons
**Sauté:** In the same skillet (wiped clean), melt 30 ml of butter over medium-high heat. Sauté the mushroom halves until golden and tender, about 4-5 minutes. Season with salt and pepper.

**Photo:** `magrets-de-canard-pommes-sarladaises-step-4.jpg` - Golden sautéed mushrooms in skillet

---

### Step 5: Serve / Servir
**Slice and plate:** Slice the rested duck breasts on the bias into 1 cm thick slices. Arrange on warm plates alongside the Sarladaise potatoes and sautéed mushrooms. Drizzle generously with the red wine and shallot sauce.

> **Chef's Tip:** For an elegant presentation, fan the duck slices and spoon the sauce partially over the meat, letting the crispy skin stay visible.

**Photo:** `magrets-de-canard-pommes-sarladaises-final.jpg` - Sliced duck breast with crispy skin, golden potatoes, mushrooms, and glossy sauce

## Chef's Tips & Variations / Tips y Variaciones

### Tips:
1. **Scoring the skin:** Cut deep enough to reach the meat but not into it — this helps render the fat and creates a beautiful crispy texture.
2. **Cold pan start:** Always start duck breasts in a cold pan fat-side down for the best results.
3. **Resting is crucial:** 10 minutes of resting ensures juicy, evenly pink meat.

### Variations:
- **With foie gras:** Add a small slice of pan-seared foie gras on top for an ultra-luxurious version.
- **Different mushrooms:** Chanterelles or morels in season are exquisite alternatives.
- **Fruit pairing:** Add a cherry or fig compote on the side for a sweet counterpoint.

### Make Ahead:
> The sauce can be made up to 2 days in advance and reheated. The potatoes are best made fresh but can be kept warm in a low oven for up to 30 minutes.

### Leftovers:
> Leftover duck can be sliced cold for salads or reheated gently in the oven at 150°C/300°F. Do not microwave — it will ruin the crispy skin. Leftover potatoes reheat well in a hot skillet.

## History & Background / Historia y Contexto

Les pommes de terre sarladaises tirent leur nom de la ville de Sarlat-la-Canéda, au cœur du Périgord noir en Dordogne. Cette région est célèbre pour sa gastronomie riche, notamment le canard, le foie gras et la truffe. Les pommes sarladaises sont traditionnellement cuites dans la graisse de canard ou d'oie, un ingrédient abondant dans cette région d'élevage de volailles.

Le magret de canard — la poitrine d'un canard engraissé pour le foie gras — est devenu un plat emblématique de la cuisine du Sud-Ouest de la France. C'est le chef André Daguin qui a popularisé le magret grillé dans les années 1960 à Auch, dans le Gers. Avant cela, cette pièce de viande était principalement confite.

L'association du magret poêlé avec les pommes sarladaises et une sauce au vin rouge est devenue un classique des tables de fête françaises, particulièrement à Noël et au Nouvel An.

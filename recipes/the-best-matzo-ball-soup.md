## Ingredients

- 4 large eggs , beaten
- 1/4 cup ( 60 ml ) seltzer or water (see notes)
- 1/4 cup ( 60 ml ) Schmaltz (rendered chicken fat) or neutral oil like vegetable or canola oil (see notes)
- 1 cup matzo meal ( 4 ounces ; 113 g )
- 1/8 to 1 teaspoon baking powder (optional; see notes)
- 2 1/2 teaspoons Diamond Crystal kosher salt , divided; for table salt, use half as much by volume
- 1/4 teaspoon freshly ground black pepper
- 3 quarts ( 2.8 L ) good-quality homemade chicken stock , divided
- 1/2 cup finely diced carrot ( 7 ounces ; 198 g ), from 1 large carrot
- 1/2 cup finely diced celery ( 2 3/4 ounces ; 78 g ), from 1 large celery rib
- 2 fresh dill sprigs , plus picked fresh dill fronds for garnish


## Instructions

1. Meanwhile, divide stock evenly between two pots, season both with 1/2 teaspoon salt each, and bring to a simmer over medium heat. Add diced carrot, celery, and dill sprigs to one pot and simmer until just tender, about 30 minutes; discard dill and cover to keep warm.

2. Using wet hands, and re-wetting hands as necessary, form matzo-ball mixture into 1- to 1 1/2–inch balls, and add to the simmering pot of stock without the carrots and celery. When all matzo balls have been added, cover and simmer until matzo balls have expanded and cooked through, about 40 minutes. Cooked matzo balls can be kept warm in their broth until ready to serve.

3. Using a slotted spoon, transfer matzo balls to serving bowls (you should have at least 3 per bowl); strain matzo-ball-cooking broth and save for another use. Ladle hot clear broth with carrots and celery into each bowl and garnish with dill fronds. Serve.

4. If you want dense sinkers, use water and omit the baking powder. For light matzo balls that still have some substance, use seltzer but omit the baking powder. For very light matzo balls use seltzer and add anywhere from 1/8 teaspoon to 1 full teaspoon baking powder (more baking powder will yield increasingly light matzo balls, with the higher amounts making insanely light ones).

5. Schmaltz will add the most flavor to the matzo balls, but takes time to prepare; neutral oils are easier but don&#39;t add flavor.

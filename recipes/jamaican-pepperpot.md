# Jamaican Pepperpot

> This hearty soup, rich with slow-simmered greens, is perfect for laid-back weekend entertaining.

**Author:** Michelle and Suzanne Rousseau

## Ingredients

- 8 ounces ( 227 g ) pickled pig tail , cut into 1-inch segments
- 4 ounces ( 113 g ) cured salt beef , salt pork, or salted pig&#39;s tail, cut into 1-inch pieces (see notes)
- 4 ounces ( 113 g ) beef chuck , cut into 1-inch cubes (optional)
- 1 large bunch callaloo ( 1 1/2 to 2 pounds ; 680-910 g ), see notes
- 1 pound ( 450 g ) coco heart (taro leaves), kale , or spinach, rinsed well (see notes)
- 1 pound ( 450 g ) yellow yam , peeled and diced into 1-inch cubes (see notes)
- 1 pound ( 450 g ) coco ( taro root), peeled and cut into 1-inch cubes (see notes)
- 12 pieces okra ( 6 ounces ; 170 g ), cut into 1-inch lengths
- 1 green bell pepper (about 5 ounces; 142 g ), stemmed, seeded, and diced
- Kosher salt
- 2 whole Scotch bonnet peppers
- 3 scallions , root ends trimmed
- 3 springs fresh thyme
- 1 medium clove garlic , thinly sliced
- 1/2 cup ( 118 ml ) coconut milk from 1 (13.5-ounce; 400 ml) can
- Freshly ground black pepper
- 4 ounces ( 113 g ) janga ( Jamaican freshwater shrimp ; substitute crayfish or 16/20 shrimp), optional
- 1 1/2 cups all-purpose flour (6 3/4 ounces ; 191 g )
- 1/2 cup ( 118 ml ) water
- Buttered sliced Jamaican hardo bread , coco bread, or thin cassava wafers, for serving
- Pickapeppa sauce , for serving


## Instructions

1. For the Soup: Place pig tail and cured salt beef in a large bowl and cover with ample cold water. Cover, transfer to refrigerator, and let soak for at least 2 hours or up to 12 hours. Drain and rinse.

2. In a large stock pot or Dutch oven, combine pickled pig tail, cured salt beef, and beef chuck, (if using). Cover meat with 3 1/2 quarts (910g) cold water and bring to a boil over high heat. Cover, reduce heat, and simmer (adjusting heat as needed to maintain simmer) until meat is tender, 1 1/2 to 2 hours.

3. Meanwhile, cut any woody stems from callaloo leaves and separate leaves from stalks; discard stalks. Transfer leaves to a medium bowl, add 1 teaspoon salt, then fill bowl with cold water and stir vigorously to wash well of all debris or sand. Drain well, then repeat rinsing steps if needed until water is clear.

4. Once meat is tender, add callaloo and coco heart to Dutch oven and simmer until greens are wilted and tender, about 15 minutes. Using a spider skimmer or slotted spoon, remove cooked callaloo and coco heart and transfer to a blender jar along with 1 cup (237 ml) cooking liquid. Pulse mixture in blender to a coarse (but not fully smooth) puree. Set aside.

5. Add yam, coco, okra, and green pepper to Dutch oven and continue to simmer, adjusting heat as needed.

6. For the Spinners (Dumplings): Meanwhile, in a small bowl, whisk together flour and a large pinch of salt. Add 1/2 cup (118ml) cold water and knead until a dough ball forms. Let rest 15 minutes. Tear off small pieces of dough and roll into cigarette-sized strips. Stir shaped spinners (dumplings) into Dutch oven with Scotch bonnet peppers, scallions, thyme, and garlic. Return to simmer.

7. Once simmering, add pureed greens to Dutch oven and continue to cook for 30 minutes. Stir in coconut milk and cook until warmed through and soup has thickened slightly, about 15 minutes. Stir in janga or shrimp, if using, and simmer until opaque and just cooked through, about 3 minutes. Season pepperpot with salt and pepper to taste.

8. For Serving: Remove Scotch bonnet peppers, scallions, and thyme sprigs. Serve pepperpot with buttered sliced hardo bread, coco bread, or thin cassava wafers (which can be found in Caribbean grocery stores), and Pickapeppa sauce.

9. Large stock pot or Dutch oven with lid, blender

10. A pressure cooker may be used in place of the Dutch oven in this recipe. If using a pressure cooker, in Step 2, cover pot, bring to high pressure, and cook under pressure for 30 minutes. Carefully release pressure, and continue with Step 3.

11. Cured salt beef, is a corned beef–like product sold in Jamaican and Caribbean markets, as are salt pork and salted pig tail.

12. Callaloo is a Caribbean term for some types of leafy greens, but it can refer to different plants depending on the context. In Jamaica, amaranth leaves are often used, but taro (dasheen) leaves can also work. In a pinch, you can substitute kale or collard greens as well.

13. Coco heart are the heart-shaped leaves of the coco (taro) plant.

14. Coco is the Jamaican name for taro root. You can also use eddoes, a relative of taro.

15. Yellow yams are available at Caribbean and some African grocers. To cut yellow yams, rinse well under running water to remove any dirt. Rub 1 teaspoon oil on hands (to form a light protective barrier from the slimy yam flesh) and peel. Immediately transfer to a bowl of salted cold water to prevent oxidation. Working with one yam at a time, dice, then return to salted water until ready to use.

16. Pepperpot can be refrigerated in an airtight container for up to 3 days.

# Beef Nilaga (Filipino Beef Soup)

> Each bite of beef nilaga’s melt-in-your-mouth meat, buttery bits of bone marrow and tendon, and unctuous vegetable-laden beef broth is a symphony of textures and flavors. Here, Philippine-born chef Yana Gilbuena shares her recipe for the Filipino beef soup.

## Ingredients

- 5 pounds ( 2.25 kg ) beef bones , cut into roughly 2-inch discs (see notes)
- Neutral oil such as vegetable or canola oil for brushing the bones
- 6 scallions , chopped into 2-inch pieces
- One 2-inch piece of fresh ginger
- 6 cloves garlic , lightly smashed
- 4 pounds ( 1.8 Kg ) beef shanks , bone in
- 4 teaspoons Diamond Crystal kosher salt , divided, plus more to taste; for table salt, use half as much by volume
- 1/2 tablespoon freshly ground black pepper
- 7 tablespoons (105ml) neutral oil such as canola or vegetable, divided
- 1 pound ( 453 g ) beef tendons , cut into 2-inch long pieces
- 1 leek , trimmed, green tops chopped and white and light green bottoms minced (see notes)
- 1/4 cup ( 25 g ) diced ginger
- 2 tablespoons (about 6 cloves ) crushed and finely chopped garlic , divided
- 2 ears of corn , husked
- 1 pound ( 453 g ) yellow potatoes , cut into 1-inch pieces
- 1/2 head green cabbage (about 1 pound ; 453 g ), halved, cored, and cut into 1-inch chunks
- 1 pound ( 453 g ) baby bok choy , ends trimmed, and halved through core
- 1 medium yellow onion ( 8 ounces ; 226 g ), chopped
- 1/4 cup ( 60 ml ) fish sauce , plus more to taste
- 1 scallion , sliced thin on the bias for garnish


## Instructions

1. For the beef stock: Fill a large stockpot halfway with cold water. Add bones and bring to a boil over high heat until you see bubbles forming at the top and the blood and other impurities are drawn out of the bones, about 5 minutes. Drain the bones to rinse off any remaining scum, then pat dry.

2. Transfer roasted beef bones to a stovetop or electric pressure cooker (such as an Instant Pot). Pour off and discard (or save for another use) any accumulated fat from the pan. Pour a thin layer of boiling water into the still-hot roasting pan and scrape up any browned bits. Pour pan juices into the pressure cooker. Add enough cold water to bring level to the cooker&#39;s max-fill line; do not let water exceed the max-fill line, even if some solids are not submerged.

3. Close pressure cooker lid, bring to high pressure, and cook for 2 hours 30 minutes. Allow cooker to depressurize naturally. Carefully open cooker and strain stock through a fine-mesh strainer into a large pot. Discard solids, though if there&#39;s shank meat in there, be sure to reserve it to add to the soup. (At this stage the broth can be cooled and refrigerated for up to 5 days or frozen for up to 3 months before continuing with the recipe.)

4. For the fried garlic: In a small skillet, heat remaining 3 tablespoons oil and remaining 1 1/2 tablespoons garlic over medium-high and cook, stirring frequently, until garlic is crispy and brown, 3 to 5 minutes. Set aside.

5. For finishing the soup: To the large soup pot with the beef stock and meat, add the leek bottoms, cabbage, bok choy, onion, and fish sauce and bring to a gentle boil over medium-high heat. Reduce heat to medium-low and simmer until onions, cabbage, and bok choy soften and turn translucent, about 20 minutes. Season broth to taste with extra fish sauce or salt, if needed.

6. Stir in the corn and cook until warmed through, about 5 minutes. Portion the meat and divide the soup evenly into individual bowls, topping with portions of the roasted potatoes. Garnish with scallions and prepared fried garlic with oil and serve.

7. Large stock pot or Dutch oven, pressure cooker , strainer, small skillet

8. For the broth, make sure the beef bones are cut short enough to fit into your pressure cooker (ask your butcher to cut them shorter if not). You can use any assortment of bones your butcher has, but bones with bits of meat still attached will give more flavor, while connective tissue–rich options like joints and feet will deliver more gelatin to the stock; you can also include some veal bones for even more gelatin. A cross-cut beef shank, while not required, will boost the flavor even more; add the meat to the soup so it doesn&#39;t go to waste.

9. I recommend saving the trimmings when you prepare the cabbage, bok choy, leeks, ginger, and onion; you can use them (as well as the roasted corn cobs) to make a vegetable stock for another use.

10. If you prefer to use boneless shanks, cut into 2-inch cubes and follow the recipe as written for bone-in shanks.

11. If you like, you can use other vegetables, such as green beans, carrots, napa cabbage, or mushrooms instead of or in addition to the vegetables in the recipe. Monitor their simmering time and cook until they are just tender.
